About

I am a PhD student in the Electrical and Computer Engineering (ECE) Department at Purdue University. My work has involved satellite image change detection, multivariate time-series forecasting on sensor data, and model compression for edge AI.

Although AI and machine learning have become increasingly prominent, modern models remain inefficient for real-world edge deployment. Large-scale systems such as ChatGPT require significant computational power, memory, and energy, making them difficult to deploy in resource-constrained environments. Engineers need to make things more efficienct - This is the reason behind my interest in model compression and efficient learning strategies that preserve performance while reducing computational cost.

At the same time, I believe we still lack a fundamental understanding of how models should learn and reason about information over time (in other words, temporal reasoning). While many methods exist for temporal modeling, truly understanding dynamic scenes and evolving visual content remains an open problem. Through my research, I aim to contribute toward building models that better capture temporal structure and can be deployed effectively on edge devices.

Overall, I try to build efficient and interpretable models that can operate reliably in real-world environments.

Problems I’m Interested In

My research interests are driven by fundamental problems that arise when deploying learning-based systems in real-world, resource-constrained, and temporally evolving environments. Rather than focusing solely on model performance in isolation, I am interested in understanding why models fail, what information they rely on, and how they can be made more efficient, interpretable, and reliable.

One major problem I care about is efficient learning under limited computational budgets. Modern deep learning models are often over-parameterized and expensive, making them unsuitable for edge deployment. I am interested in identifying which components of a model are truly necessary, how redundancy can be reduced, and how inductive structure can be imposed without sacrificing performance.

Another core challenge is temporal reasoning and dependency modeling. Many real-world systems—such as satellite imagery streams and multivariate sensor data—exhibit complex temporal dependencies that are not well captured by static models. I am interested in understanding how information propagates over time, how causality and influence can be disentangled across channels, and how models can learn meaningful temporal representations rather than short-term correlations.

I am also interested in the problem of change understanding, particularly in visual data. Beyond detecting whether a change has occurred, I care about understanding what changed, where it changed, and why that change is meaningful. This includes problems such as separating semantic changes from nuisance factors like illumination or seasonal variation, and guiding models to focus on informative regions rather than background noise.

Finally, I am interested in bridging classical signal processing with modern learning-based approaches. Many inverse problems and decomposition-based formulations offer interpretability and theoretical grounding that purely data-driven models lack. I am motivated by problems where combining these perspectives can lead to more robust, explainable, and controllable systems.
